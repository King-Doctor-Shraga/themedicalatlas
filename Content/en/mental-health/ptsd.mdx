## Overview: Understanding PTSD

Post-traumatic stress disorder develops after exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. PTSD involves intrusive memories, avoidance, negative mood changes, and hyperarousal. Trauma affects the brain, body, and emotional regulation. Recovery is possible with appropriate treatment <CitationRef index={1} />.

PTSD is not a sign of weakness; it's a normal response to abnormal events that continues longer than expected.

<InfoBox title="Key Information">
PTSD symptoms cluster into four categories: intrusive symptoms (flashbacks, nightmares), avoidance (avoiding reminders), negative cognitions and mood (guilt, emotional numbness), and arousal (hypervigilance, startled easily). Symptoms must persist for over one month and cause functional impairment.
</InfoBox>

## Trauma Responses

Flashbacks make past trauma feel present. Nightmares disturb sleep. Avoidance of trauma reminders limits activities and relationships. Hypervigilance keeps the nervous system in constant threat detection. Depression, anger, and emotional numbness occur frequently. Some experience dissociation or depersonalization.

## Trauma-Focused Therapies

Cognitive processing therapy helps change trauma-related thoughts and beliefs. Prolonged exposure therapy gradually reintroduces trauma reminders in safe context. Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) processes trauma memories using bilateral stimulation. Narrative therapy develops coherent trauma story.

## Pharmacological Treatment

SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine) reduce PTSD symptoms effectively. Medications address co-occurring depression and anxiety. Prazosin reduces nightmares in some individuals <CitationRef index={2} />.

<WarningBox>
Suicidal ideation frequently accompanies PTSD; immediate intervention is required. Substance use often develops as trauma coping; integrated treatment addresses both conditions. Avoid retraumatization; adequate preparation and pacing support therapy engagement.
</WarningBox>

<ClinicalNote>
Trauma-informed care recognizes trauma's pervasive impact and prioritizes safety and choice. Therapy should be evidence-based and trauma-focused. Support groups connect trauma survivors. Peer support specialists offer invaluable guidance. Combining therapy and medication shows best outcomes.
</ClinicalNote>

## Self-Care and Recovery

Grounding techniques manage flashbacks and anxiety. Regular exercise supports nervous system regulation. Sleep hygiene addresses trauma-related sleep disruption. Social connection, creative expression, and meaning-making support healing. Recovery is possible; professional support facilitates the journey.
